# How I Vet a New Frozen Seafood Supplier from China in 5 Minutes.

> **Adapted from:** 博客文章《冷冻海鲜进口指南：怎么判断中国供应商靠不靠谱》(2026-05-26)
> **Style:** First-person, LinkedIn thought leadership, practical trade knowledge
> **Target length:** 1,500-2,000 characters English

---

Working with frozen seafood sourcing teaches you one thing quickly: an inbox full of FOB quotes does not tell you who is real, who understands the product, and who may ship you a container of frozen disappointment.

Here's the thing. HACCP certificates are table stakes — every export plant has one. A BRC certificate? That's better, but only if it's Grade A and current. What separates a reliable supplier from a time bomb usually shows up in the small things.

Here's my personal checklist. Four steps. Five minutes each. Anyone can do this.

## Step 1: The Certificate Test (Don't Stop at "Yes, We Have It")

Ask for a PDF of their HACCP or BRC certificate. Not a screenshot. Not a WeChat photo. The actual PDF.

Then open it in Adobe Acrobat. Click the signature panel. Is the digital signature valid? You'd be shocked how many "certificates" floating around are just a scanned piece of paper someone tweaked in Photoshop.

Also check the issuing body. SGS, Intertek, Bureau Veritas — these carry weight. A certificate from a local agency you've never heard of? That's not necessarily fake, but it's not the same thing either.

If you're selling to European retail, the difference between BRC Grade A and Grade B is not academic. A-grade plants have passed unannounced audits. That's the difference between a factory that's always ready and one that cleans up for inspection day.

## Step 2: The Three-Batch Sample

Everyone sends a perfect sample. I could hand-pick 10 beautiful mackerel from any production line in Shandong.

What I want to know is: can they do it twice more?

Ask for samples from three different production batches. If the supplier hesitates — "they're all the same quality" — that's your signal. Consistency issues.

When the samples arrive, do your own work. Weigh them frozen. Thaw them. Weigh them again. Calculate the glaze percentage yourself. I've seen samples with 8% glaze that somehow became 25% on the commercial shipment. The math on that container changes fast.

One more thing: ask for third-party lab results. SGS or Eurofins. If they only show you their in-house lab report, treat it as a starting point, not proof.

## Step 3: The Chat Test

This is my favorite, and it works in any language.

Ask the salesperson a specific question: "Which vessel did this batch come off?" or "What's the catch area on the health certificate?"

Someone who works at or closely with the factory can answer that in five seconds. A middleman who's never been to the cold storage will start hedging: "Pacific Ocean," "northern waters." You'll hear the difference immediately.

Also: ask what happens if the container arrives with short weight or a quality claim. A real exporter will walk you through the protocol: photos within 48 hours, joint survey, replacement or price adjustment options. An amateur will say "don't worry, it never happens."

One more trick I've learned: if someone replies to your WeChat instantly at 10:00 PM Beijing time, they're probably a trading company. Factory people have lives. They go home.

## Step 4: The Price Sanity Check

May 2026, Pacific mackerel 300/500g whole round IQF, FOB Qingdao/Yantai: $800–$1,200 per metric ton.

If someone quotes you $500, there are exactly two explanations. One: the fish are undersized or poorly graded. Two: they're not the first source and they'll find a way to make up the margin later — extra documentation fees, container charges, something.

Prices that are too low are as dangerous as prices that are too high. Know the market range before you negotiate.
