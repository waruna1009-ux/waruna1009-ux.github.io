# How China's Frozen Seafood Export Industry is Adapting to 2026

If you're importing frozen seafood in 2026, you've already noticed: the rules have changed. Tariffs that spooked the market in 2025 have largely settled. But new pressures — from sustainability mandates to carbon accounting — are reshaping sourcing decisions faster than most buyers expected.

Here's what's actually happening on the ground in Shandong, China's seafood processing heartland.

## The tariff picture: Stabilized, but not simple

The panic is over. S&P Global noted in March 2026 that global seafood supply chains have adapted to the post-2025 tariff landscape. Direct China-to-US shipments still carry friction, but alternative routes — through Southeast Asian processors, through Middle Eastern distributors — are functioning smoothly.

For the Chinese exporters I work with daily, the focus has shifted from "how do we get around tariffs" to "which markets are growing fastest?" The answer: West Africa, Southeast Asia, and the Middle East.

## The mackerel moment

Here's a concrete example. Norwegian mackerel prices hit yet another record high in January 2026. For European canneries and Japanese processors that traditionally relied on North Atlantic catch, the math has stopped working.

China's Pacific mackerel (Scomber japonicus) — landed in Shandong, processed in HACCP-certified plants, shipped from Qingdao — is filling the gap. FOB prices in the $1,800-2,500/MT range (300/500g, whole round, IQF) are attracting buyers who never sourced from China before.

At my company, we've seen inquiries from European buyers increase 3x compared to this time last year. They're not just price-shopping — they're discovering that Chinese processing quality, when sourced from the right plants, is fully competitive with any market.

## What buyers should look for in 2026

If you're sourcing frozen seafood from China this year:

1. **Ask for BRC Grade A, not just HACCP.** HACCP is table stakes. BRC certification at Grade A level tells you the plant operates at European retail standards.

2. **Don't judge by sample alone.** Request samples from three different production batches. A supplier who hesitates likely has consistency issues.

3. **Watch the carbon conversation.** European supermarket chains are already asking suppliers for carbon footprint data. Chinese plants that invest in energy-efficient freezing and cold chain will be positioned to win those contracts.

4. **West Africa and Southeast Asia are the growth markets.** If your sourcing strategy is US/EU-only, you're leaving margin on the table. The demand in Lagos, Accra, Manila, and Bangkok is real and growing.

The bottom line: 2026 is not a year of crisis for seafood trade. It's a year of recalibration. Buyers who update their supplier playbook — looking beyond traditional origins, asking harder questions about certifications, and diversifying their market exposure — will come out ahead.
