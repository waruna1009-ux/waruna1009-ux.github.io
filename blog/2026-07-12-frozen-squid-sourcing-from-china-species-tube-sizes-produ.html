Frozen Squid Sourcing from China: Species, Tube Sizes, Product Forms & Quality FAQ

Frozen Squid Sourcing from China: Species, Tube Sizes, Product Forms & Quality FAQ

**Disclaimer**: This guide is based on common seafood trade knowledge and industry practice. Product specifications, quality standards, and regulatory requirements should be confirmed per batch, per buyer, and per destination market. Nothing in this guide constitutes commercial or regulatory advice.

1. What squid species does China supply, and what are they used for?

China's squid processing industry works primarily with three main species sourced through the country's large distant-water fishing fleet:

| Common Trade Name | Scientific Name | Typical Characteristics | Common End Uses |

|---|---|---|---|

| Argentine Shortfin Squid | Illex argentinus | Thicker meat, good texture, firm flesh | Tubes, rings, tentacles; foodservice & retail |

| Jumbo Flying Squid / Humboldt Squid | Dosidicus gigas | Larger size, coarser muscle, more affordable | Rings, strips, block products; further processing |

| Indian Squid / Mitre Squid | Loligo chinensis (formerly Uroteuthis chinensis) | Smaller, more delicate | Light processing; mid-to-premium foodservice |

How the supply chain works: China's distant-water fishing vessels operate in the Southwest Atlantic (Argentina fishery), Southeast Pacific (Peru fishery), and high seas. Catches are partially processed at sea then landed at Chinese ports (Qingdao, Yantai, Dalian, Zhoushan) for further processing — cleaning, sorting, cutting, freezing, and packing. Some raw frozen whole squid is also imported for re-processing. Finished products are then exported globally.

Note: Species availability and catch volumes vary by fishing season and quota. Confirm current supply with your supplier.

2. What are the common squid product forms?

Squid can be processed into many forms depending on the buyer's end market and application:

| Product Form | Description | Typical Applications |

|---|---|---|

| Whole Round | Whole squid, frozen — with or without guts (cleaned or uncleaned) | Further processing; Asian & Middle Eastern markets |

| Tube (Body) | Squid body tube only — skin-on or skinless | Rings, stuffing; foodservice & retail |

| Rings | Cross-cut tube pieces at specified diameter & thickness | Calamari rings — restaurant, frozen food, retail |

| Tentacles | Full tentacle set, half-cut, or cut pieces | Appetizers, mixed seafood, snacks |

| Strips | Flattened tube cut into strips | Fast food, ready meals, stir-fry |

| Wings & Fins | Side pieces trimmed from the body | Further processing, minced products |

| Skewered | Tube/ring/tentacle pieces on skewers | BBQ, grilling, foodservice |

| Whole Cleaned | Gutted, beak removed, body cleaned — frozen whole | Stuffing, grilling, roasting |

Note: Not every supplier offers all forms. Confirm product form availability and minimum order quantities per supplier.

3. How are squid tubes sized?

Squid tubes are most commonly graded by length (cm):

| Grade | Tube Length | Typical Use |

|---|---|---|

| 10–15 cm | Smaller tubes | Rings, salads, mixed seafood |

| 15–20 cm | Medium tubes | Rings, stuffing, foodservice |

| 20–25 cm | Large tubes | Premium rings, stuffing, grilling |

| 25–30+ cm | Jumbo tubes | Steaks, further processing |

Rings are specified by both diameter (cm) and thickness (mm). Common diameters: 1-2 cm, 2-3 cm, 3-4 cm. Ring thickness is typically 6-12 mm depending on market preference.

Tentacles can be supplied as:

  • Full tentacle set (all arms intact)
  • Half-cut
  • Cut pieces at specified length

Whole round squid is typically graded by pieces per kg (pcs/kg), with different ranges for different species.

Note: Sizing standards may vary slightly between processors. Always confirm exact specifications with your supplier.

4. What packing options are available for frozen squid?

The choice of packing method depends on the buyer's downstream use, logistics, and market segment:

| Pack Type | Description | Best For | Typical Pack Weight |

|---|---|---|---|

| IQF (Individually Quick Frozen) | Each piece frozen individually, bagged in PE or vacuum + outer carton | Foodservice, retail — use what you need | 10 kg, 20 kg |

| Block Frozen | Product arranged neatly and frozen in a solid block | Bulk processing, large-scale food manufacturing | 10 kg, 20 kg, 25 kg |

| Shaterpack / Layer Pack | Loose frozen in large bulk packaging | Wholesale, supermarket warehouse | 10 kg, 20 kg |

| Vacuum Pack | Individually or portion-packed under vacuum | Premium retail, long storage | 500 g, 1 kg, 2 kg, 5 kg |

Glaze (ice glaze): A thin layer of ice applied to protect against freezer burn and dehydration during storage and transport. Glaze percentage typically ranges from 5% to 15% for squid products, though this can vary. When specifying glaze, distinguish between:

  • **Net Weight (NW):** Actual squid weight
  • **Gross Weight (GW):** Squid + glaze weight

Always confirm glaze percentage and net weight in your contract.

5. How do I assess frozen squid quality?

Here is a practical checklist that buyers commonly use:

| Parameter | What to Look For | What to Avoid |

|---|---|---|

| Appearance | White to creamy white tube; natural tentacle color | Yellowing, pinkish or gray discoloration |

| Texture | Firm, springy to the touch, intact | Soft, mushy, loose or separating |

| Thawing Yield | Typically 85-95% depending on glaze & quality | Low yield indicates excessive glaze or quality loss |

| Glaze Quality | Even ice coating, no large ice crystals | Uneven glaze, large ice crystals (indicates temperature abuse) |

| Freezer Burn | None — product should appear fresh | White or gray patches on surface |

| Odor | Clean, fresh sea smell | Ammonia, sour, metallic or rancid odor (signs of spoilage) |

| Cut Quality | Clean, consistent cuts | Ragged edges, uneven pieces, excess trimming waste |

| Packaging | Intact, properly sealed, clear labeling | Damaged bags, crushed cartons, illegible codes |

Quick test: Thaw a sample at 0-4°C (refrigerator) and check color, texture, odor and yield before finalizing a shipment.

6. What about sulphite treatment?

Some squid products (particularly peeled tubes and rings) may undergo sulphite treatment (sodium metabisulphite dip) to prevent black spotting (melanosis) and maintain a bright white appearance during frozen storage.

  • Some markets accept sulphite-treated squid within regulated limits
  • Others require **no added sulphite** (common in EU retail and some US foodservice)
  • If sulphite treatment is a concern, specify "no added sulphite" or "sulphite-free" in your purchase order

Confirm sulphite regulations applicable to your market with your customs broker or competent authorities.

7. How long does shipping take from China?

| Destination | Approximate Transit Time (from Qingdao/Ningbo) |

|---|---|

| Europe (Rotterdam, Hamburg, Barcelona) | 25–35 days |

| Middle East (Dubai, Dammam) | 15–20 days |

| Southeast Asia (Singapore, Bangkok) | 5–10 days |

| US West Coast (Los Angeles, Long Beach) | 12–18 days |

| US East Coast (New York, Savannah) | 25–30 days |

| South Korea (Busan) | 3–5 days |

| Africa (various ports) | 20–40 days |

All shipments require continuous cold chain at -18°C or below. A 40-foot reefer container typically holds approximately 20–27 metric tons of frozen squid depending on product form and packaging.

8. What certifications do I need for importing squid from China?

Required certifications depend on your destination country and market segment:

| Market | Common Requirements | Notes |

|---|---|---|

| European Union | EU-approved processing plant registration, health certificate | Check EU RASFF for current alerts |

| United States | FDA prior notice, facility registration, FSMA compliance | No mandatory country-specific certification — but importers often request third-party quality inspection |

| Middle East | Halal certification (various recognized bodies) | Confirm which Halal bodies your buyer accepts |

| Southeast Asia | Health certificate, COO | Typically less documentation than EU/US |

| South Korea | Health certificate, phytosanitary | Korea-specific import requirements apply (NYR) |

Certification requirements change. Always check with your customs broker and the competent authority in your destination country for current requirements.

9. Is squid available year-round from China?

Yes — frozen squid is available year-round from China due to the rotating catch seasons of different species:

  • **Illex argentinus** fishery: Main season Q1–Q2 (landings arrive in China Q2–Q3)
  • **Dosidicus gigas** fishery: H2 peak, supplies available year-round through frozen inventory
  • **Loligo chinensis**: Caught more consistently throughout the year in Southeast Asian waters

This seasonal rotation, combined with frozen inventory management, means most product forms are available in all months. However, pricing can fluctuate based on catch volumes, fuel costs, and global demand.
