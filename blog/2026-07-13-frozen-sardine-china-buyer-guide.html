Frozen Sardine from China: Sourcing FAQ for Importers (2026)

Answer capsule: Chinese processors supply frozen sardine (*Sardinella* spp., *Sardina pilchardus*) primarily to West Africa, Southeast Asia, and the Middle East. This guide covers species availability, standard size grades (pieces per kg), processing forms (whole round, H&G, IQF vs block-frozen), typical packing specs, and quality factors importers should verify before ordering.

1. What Sardine Species Do Chinese Processors Offer?

Several sardine species pass through Chinese processing facilities, depending on catch origin and season:

- Sardinella aurita (round sardinella) — widely available, common in West African and Mediterranean waters; a staple in Chinese-origin frozen sardine exports.

- Sardinella longiceps (Indian oil sardine) — important for Southeast Asian and Middle Eastern buyers; often sourced from Indian Ocean fisheries.

- Sardina pilchardus (European pilchard) — available in smaller volumes; typically from Northeast Atlantic fisheries.

- Other Sardinella species — may appear seasonally depending on regional catch availability.

Note for importers: Always confirm the exact species and origin with your supplier before contracting. Different species can have different fat content, texture, and shelf-life characteristics.

2. How Are Frozen Sardines Graded by Size?

Sardines are graded by pieces per kilogram (pcs/kg) — smaller numbers mean larger fish. The most common commercial grades include:

Size Grade Pieces per kg Typical Use
6-8 pcs/kg Large Canning (premium), grilling
8-10 pcs/kg Medium-Large Canning, direct retail
10-12 pcs/kg Medium Canning, fishmeal/bait
12-15 pcs/kg Small-Medium Fishmeal, bait, animal feed
16-20+ pcs/kg Small Fishmeal, reduction
Which size sells best in your market?

- West Africa: 8-10 and 10-12 pcs/kg are most popular for retail and canning.

- Southeast Asia: 10-12 and 12-15 pcs/kg for processing and food service.

- Middle East: 8-10 pcs/kg for direct consumption and grilling markets.

3. Whole Round vs H&G vs Dressed — Which Processing Type?

Type Description Best For
Whole Round (WR) Fish frozen whole, ungutted Lowest cost; canning, fishmeal, bait
Headed & Gutted (H&G) Head and viscera removed Retail, food service, value-added processing
Dressed Head, viscera, tail, fins removed Ready-to-cook retail; higher value
IQF (Individually Quick Frozen) Each fish frozen separately Premium retail; easy portioning, less clumping
Block Frozen Fish frozen in solid blocks (10kg/20kg) Bulk shipping; traditional packing for canning and processing

Most frozen sardine exports from China ship as block-frozen whole round in 10kg or 20kg cartons. IQF is available but typically at a premium.

4. What Is the Typical Packing?

- Carton weight: 10 kg or 20 kg net weight (depending on buyer preference and shipping economics).

- Inner packaging: Polyethylene (PE) bag inside a master carton.

- Glaze (ice protection): Standard glaze weight should be confirmed in the contract. Buyers should agree on net weight vs. glazed weight terms upfront.

- Palletization: Standard export pallets; container loading plan should be confirmed per order.

5. What Factors Influence the FOB Price of Frozen Sardine?

FOB pricing for frozen sardine fluctuates with:

- Species and size grade — larger fish (lower pcs/kg) generally command higher prices.

- Catch season and availability — seasonal fishery closures and catch quotas affect supply.

- Processing form — whole round is cheapest; dressed and IQF carry premiums.

- Shipping and logistics — freight rates, container availability, and port conditions.

- Order volume and contract terms — larger volumes may benefit from scale efficiencies.

Practical note: Pricing is confirmed per order based on current market conditions, species, specification, and shipping terms. Contact us with your target spec and destination for a current indication.

6. What Should I Check for Quality?

Visual inspection (at loading or arrival):

- Uniform size within the declared grade

- No excessive ice crystal formation or freezer burn

- Cartons intact, dry, and properly labeled

Key quality risks:

- Histamine: Sardines are scombroid-adjacent and can develop histamine if cold chain breaks. Proper temperature control from catch through processing and shipping is essential.

- Dehydration / freezer burn: Indicates temperature fluctuation during storage or transport.

- Foreign matter or broken fish: Indicates rough handling or poor grading.

Pre-loading inspection can be arranged according to buyer requirements. Documentation including health certificates, origin certificates, and catch certificates are confirmed per product, partner plant, and destination market.

7. FAQ: Common Questions

Q: What is the MOQ for frozen sardine?

A: MOQ varies by supplier and product specification. A typical trial order may start with one 20-foot or 40-foot container (approximately 25-27 MT for a 40-foot reefer). Smaller mixed-container loads may be possible — discuss with your supplier.

Q: What is the shelf life of frozen sardine?

A: Typically 24 months from production date when stored at -18°C or below, depending on packaging and glaze. Always verify the production date and storage conditions on arrival.

Q: What is the HS code for frozen sardine?

A: Frozen sardines generally fall under HS 0303.53 (Sardines, sardinella, brisling or sprats, frozen). Confirm exact classification with your customs broker for your destination country.

Q: How are frozen sardines shipped to Africa / Middle East / Southeast Asia?

A: In refrigerated containers (reefers) at -18°C or below. Transit time depends on the origin port (commonly Qingdao, Shanghai, or other major Chinese ports) and destination. Shipping arrangements and documentation are confirmed per order.

Q: What is the seasonal availability?

A: Catch seasons vary by species and fishing zone. Chinese processors often source from multiple fisheries to maintain supply across seasons. Confirm current availability with your supplier when placing orders.

Q: Do I need an import license or special permits?

A: Requirements vary by country. Many African and Middle Eastern markets require standard import documentation (health certificate, certificate of origin, bill of lading, commercial invoice, packing list). EU markets have additional catch certificate requirements. Check with your local import authority or customs broker.
