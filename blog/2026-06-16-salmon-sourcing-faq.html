Frozen Salmon Sourcing FAQ: 6 Key Fields Every Importer Should Clarify Before Requesting a Quote

Answer capsule: When sourcing frozen salmon from China, specifying the correct species (Atlantic vs. Pacific), product form (fillet, portion, trim, belly), piece weight, fat content preference, packing method, and document requirements in your initial inquiry can reduce back-and-forth and help suppliers give you a more accurate quote. This FAQ covers the 6 fields that matter most for importers, distributors, and processors.

Why This Matters

Frozen salmon is one of the most traded seafood categories globally, and China plays an increasingly active role in salmon processing and re-export. Whether you are a Southeast Asian processor looking for raw material, a European distributor building your salmon program, or a first-time buyer exploring Chinese suppliers — getting the inquiry right saves time on both sides.

Below are the 6 key fields every importer should clarify before requesting a quote.

Field 1: Species — Atlantic vs. Pacific Salmon

Q: Should I specify the salmon species in my inquiry?

A: Yes — and it matters more than most buyers realize.

  • Atlantic salmon (Salmo salar): The most commonly farmed species globally. Known for consistent fat content, mild flavor, and orange-pink flesh. Preferred by European and North American markets for fresh/frozen fillets, steaks, and sashimi-grade portions. HS Code 0303.13.
  • Pacific salmon species: Includes Chinook, Coho, Sockeye, Pink, and Chum. Wild-caught, with more varied fat content, flesh color, and firmness depending on the species and season. Often used for canning, smoking, or further processing.

Why it matters: Atlantic and Pacific salmon differ in fat profile, texture, price range, and processing suitability. If you send a generic "salmon fillet" inquiry, the supplier may quote the wrong species — and the price gap between Atlantic and certain Pacific species can be significant.

How to specify in your inquiry: State the species name (e.g., "Atlantic Salmon, Salmo salar") and your intended use (e.g., "for HORECA fillet portions" or "for smoking").

Field 2: Product Form — Fillet, Portion, Trim, Skin, Belly

Q: What product forms are commonly available for frozen salmon?

A: Frozen salmon is typically available in these forms, each serving different downstream applications:

Product Form Description Typical Applications
Whole RoundIntact fish, unguttedProcessing plants, smoking
H&G (Headed & Gutted)Head and viscera removedFurther processing, portioning
Skin-on FilletFull fillet with skinRetail, food service
Skinless FilletFull fillet without skinProcessed products, ready-to-cook
PortionPre-cut fillet pieces, uniform weightHORECA, chain restaurants
TrimIrregular pieces from filletingValue-added processing, minced products
BellyFatty belly sectionPremium grilling, sashimi
SkinSalmon skinSnacks (e.g., salmon skin crisps)

Why it matters: Each form serves a different market and price point. Trim and belly have distinct price/value profiles compared to center-cut fillets. Specifying the form up front prevents mismatched quotations.

Q: I'm a processor — should I ask for H&G or fillet?

A: It depends on your processing line. H&G gives you full control over portioning and yield. Pre-cut fillets or portions reduce your labor cost but come at a higher unit price. Some buyers prefer block-frozen fillets for consistent portioning, while others use IQF portions for retail-ready packing.

Field 3: Piece Weight and Thickness

Q: How important is piece weight in salmon sourcing?

A: Very important — especially for food service and retail buyers.

Common piece weight ranges for frozen salmon:

  • 4-6 oz (110-170g) — Individual portions, retail packs
  • 6-8 oz (170-225g) — Standard restaurant fillet
  • 8-10 oz (225-280g) — Large restaurant portion
  • 10+ oz (280g+) — Premium/buffet portions

For whole fish or H&G, size is typically specified by individual weight range:

  • 1-3 kg
  • 3-5 kg
  • 5+ kg

Thickness also matters for processing and cooking consistency. If you need uniform thickness for even cooking (e.g., for chain restaurants or central kitchens), mention this in your inquiry.

Field 4: Fat Content and Color Grade

Q: Should I specify fat content on my first inquiry?

A: Not always mandatory, but helpful if you have a specific requirement.

  • Higher fat content (15%+) — Preferred for grilling, sashimi, and premium retail. More pronounced marbling.
  • Medium fat (10-15%) — Versatile for most applications.
  • Lower fat (<10%) — Often used for smoking or processing where fat consistency matters less.

Color is graded on a scale (typically 20-34 on the Roche SalmoFan™). Different markets prefer different shades:

  • European retail: Typically 26-30 (medium orange)
  • Japanese sashimi: Often 30-34 (deep orange-red)
  • North American food service: 24-28

If color consistency across a container matters to you (e.g., for branded retail packs), make this clear in your inquiry.

Field 5: Packing — IQF vs. Block, Vacuum Pack, Carton Size

Q: IQF or block frozen — which is better for my market?

A: Each has advantages depending on your application.

Packing Type Advantages
IQF (Individually Quick Frozen)Easy portion control, no thawing whole blocks, preferred by food service and retail
Block FrozenMore efficient container utilization, lower per-unit freight cost, suits processing lines that thaw in bulk
Vacuum Pack (VAC)Extended shelf life, prevents freezer burn, preferred for retail-ready products
Vacuum Skin Pack (VSP)Premium presentation for retail counters

Packing weight & carton details:

  • Standard export carton: 10-20 kg net weight
  • Inner bag options: PE bag, vacuum bag, or branded retail pack
  • Carton marking: Custom marking available per distributor requirements

Q: How does packing choice affect shipping cost?

A: IQF products typically have more air space in the carton, resulting in higher freight cost per ton of actual product compared to block-frozen. If landed cost is a key factor, discuss block vs. IQF with your supplier early in the process.

Field 6: Documentation — What Documents Do I Need?

Q: What documents come with frozen salmon shipments from China?

A: Standard export documents include:

  • Health Certificate (issued by CIQ — China Inspection and Quarantine)
  • Certificate of Origin (for preferential tariff treatment where applicable)
  • Bill of Lading (ocean or rail)
  • Commercial Invoice & Packing List
  • Phytosanitary Certificate (if required by destination)

Documents required vary by destination country, product form, and processing plant. More specific certifications (EU health compliance, FDA prior notice, Halal, etc.) are checked per product, partner plant, and order.

How to specify in your inquiry: Share your destination country and intended use (e.g., "for retail sale in Poland" or "as raw material for processing in Vietnam"). This helps confirm which documents can be supported.

Summary Checklist for Your Salmon Inquiry

Before sending your first inquiry, confirm you have these 6 fields covered:

  • Species: Atlantic (Salmo salar) or Pacific — specify which
  • Product Form: Whole round / H&G / Fillet / Portion / Trim / Belly / Skin
  • Weight & Thickness: Individual piece weight range and thickness preference
  • Fat & Color: Target fat content and Roche SalmoFan color grade (if applicable)
  • Packing: IQF vs. block, vacuum pack preference, carton weight, custom marking
  • Documents: Destination country and any special document requirements
